# The Year of 70-Hour Weeks

In 1995, retail technology was still figuring itself out. Most stores ran on clunky, proprietary systems that barely talked to each other. Into that gap stepped a small company called Infosystem of NC, with a bold bet: build a modern, multi-chain point-of-sale system on cutting-edge 32-bit Visual Basic, a Microsoft SQL backend, and an OLE/COM object architecture — technology so advanced for its time that most developers hadn't touched it yet.

I was brought onto the project mid-stream, initially just to lend a hand. But within a day it was clear this wasn't a short-term favor: the project was bigger, more complex, and more urgent than anyone had let on. The team needed someone who could operate at the system level — so I was asked to stay.

---

## Building Something Special — the Team and the Product

The codebase wasn't just a cash-register app. It was a sophisticated n-tier application — close to 5,000 lines — designed to run across multiple retail chains simultaneously. Petco was deploying it as their **first-ever POS system**, and Eagles and Giant Eagle grocery stores were on the rollout list too. By the time the year was out, the system was live in over 20 stores across more than five separate chains.

For 1995, this was remarkable. Multi-tenant retail software, running on a unified architecture, deployed at that scale? The industry hadn't seen much like it.

The team reflected the ambition. When I joined, there were 8 developers; by year's end we'd nearly doubled to 15. And somewhere in that growth I was promoted to Senior Developer — the **#2 developer on the project** — the technical second-in-command of one of the most exciting retail software efforts of the time.

---

## The Cost

That year, Infosystem's POS solution won the **Microsoft Partner of the Year Award for the Retail Industry** — a recognition the whole team appreciated, but one that didn't capture what it actually cost to get there.

The developers on the team averaged nearly **70 hours a week**, most every week of that year. Not occasionally. Consistently. Week after week, the work expanded to fill every available hour — and then some. The technical achievement was real, but it was being built on the backs of people who were quietly burning out. Most of us hit 40 hours by midday Wednesday, week in and week out.

I was recognized for my contributions, winning **Rookie of the Year** on the technical side of the business — an acknowledgment that what I brought to the team mattered, and of how much I'd grown over that year.

---

## What the Award Didn't Say

There's a difference between a company that succeeds *with* its people and one that succeeds *despite* them.

I watched how this small corporation operated — how it treated the employees grinding out those 70-hour weeks, and how it treated its customers. The ethical practices I observed didn't match my personal principles. A company can win and still get the fundamentals wrong.

That realization — hard-earned and firsthand — became one of the most important lessons of my career:

> *How not to treat employees.*

Success metrics are easy to chase. Culture is harder. And when culture is broken, no award changes what it feels like to show up every day.

---

## A New Chapter

So I chose to leave and begin the next chapter of my professional life.

I started contracting — for the first time.

It wouldn't be the last.
