# Trading at the Speed of Thought

In the late 1990s, a UPS truck pulled up to a developer's home and delivered something unusual: a $20,000 workstation server and a financial satellite dish.

This was how Rabar Market Research recruited and equipped its remote engineering team — not with laptop stipends and Slack accounts, but with institutional-grade hardware dropped at your door. If you were going to help manage nearly a billion dollars in global futures markets, you were going to do it right, even from your living room.

Sonny was one of eight developers trusted with that responsibility.

---

## The Turtle's Algorithm

To understand what Rabar was building, you have to understand where Paul Rabar came from.

He was one of the original Turtles — a select group trained by legendary trader Richard Dennis in the early 1980s to settle a fundamental argument: could trading be taught? Dennis said yes. His partner William Eckhardt was skeptical. The experiment produced extraordinary results, and Rabar was among its standout graduates: a classically trained pianist who had walked away from UCLA medical school, passed through E.F. Hutton, and emerged from the Turtle experiment with a deeply internalized model of systematic, disciplined trend-following.

He didn't just follow the playbook. He built his own firm around it.

By the time Sonny joined, Rabar Market Research was operating across 20 to 50+ global futures and currency markets, managing assets approaching $1 billion, with leverage running nearly 10 times net assets. The scale was institutional. The philosophy was systematic. And the technology was the engine that made it all run.

---

## The Architecture of an Edge

The system Sonny helped build was designed around a single insight: in any market, on any given day, the 300 most liquid assets are the ones worth watching. Of those, the ones with the highest velocity and the most activity are the ones most likely to make significant corrections — and corrections are where the money is.

The software tracked those targets overnight. When markets opened, it was ready.

But speed alone wasn't the edge. The real sophistication was in the architecture. Built in C++ on a Document View framework, the system was composable — developers could swap trading models in and out like plugins. Multiple models ran simultaneously, each calibrated to different market conditions. The logic was deliberate: hedge your bets across approaches, because in any given environment, one model will likely hit. When it did, the system would weight its trades favorably. When another underperformed, it would pull back.

It was Paul Rabar's Turtle philosophy translated into code — systematic, disciplined, emotionless, and always working the probabilities.

Human analysts stayed in the loop as a final check, able to override any trade based on public news or information the models hadn't yet absorbed. But the system was the primary actor.

While Sonny was there, he also built an equities version of the same software — taking the entire commodity trading architecture and extending it to stock markets. Two asset classes. One composable system.

---

## Discipline at Home

There's a quieter story running alongside the technical one.

Remote work in the late 1990s and early 2000s wasn't a lifestyle trend — it was a logistical challenge with no established playbook. No one had figured out how to stay productive, stay connected, and stay sane while working from home on high-stakes institutional software.

Sonny was doing it with a young family. A wife. Young children. A $20,000 server humming in the background.

The discipline required to perform at that level — technically rigorous, always available, always accountable — while also being present as a father and husband was its own kind of achievement. The satellite dish on the roof didn't make it easier. It just meant the work was always there, always live, always connected to markets that didn't care what time zone you were in or whether your kid had kept you up the night before.

That season taught Sonny something that no amount of technical skill could replace: how to hold competing demands with intention, and how to show up fully in both places.

---

## The Exit

In 2003, a minor market recession gave Paul Rabar reason to end remote work arrangements and bring the team back in-house.

By any measure, Rabar himself was the kind of leader worth working for — brilliant, polite, and considerate. The end of the arrangement wasn't acrimonious. It was simply the closing of a chapter.

Sonny had spent three years inside one of the most sophisticated quantitative trading operations in the world, building C++ systems that moved institutional capital across global markets in milliseconds. He'd mastered a particular kind of software architecture — composable, model-driven, built for speed and precision.

And now the industry was shifting. C#, .NET, a new paradigm. The next chapter was already clear.

He moved on to learn it.

---

*Three years at Rabar taught Sonny what it meant to build software where the stakes were measured in billions — and what it meant to build a life where the stakes were measured in something else entirely.*
