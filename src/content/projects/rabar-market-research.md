## Problem

Rabar Market Research was a systematic futures fund built by Paul Rabar — one of the
original Turtles trained by Richard Dennis — managing assets approaching $1 billion across
20 to 50+ global futures and currency markets, with leverage running near 10x net assets.
Translating that disciplined, emotionless trend-following philosophy into software meant
building a system that could watch hundreds of markets overnight, act the instant they
opened, and never let a single model's blind spot sink the book.

## What I built

I was one of eight fully remote developers — equipped with an institutional-grade
workstation and a financial satellite dish shipped to my door — building the firm's trading
engine in C++ on a Document/View framework. The architecture was composable: trading models
plugged in and out like modules, several running at once, each calibrated to different
market conditions and weighted by how it was performing. The system tracked the ~300 most
liquid, highest-velocity assets overnight to catch the corrections where the edge lived,
while human analysts stayed in the loop to override on public news the models hadn't
absorbed. I later extended the same commodity architecture into an equities version —
one composable system spanning two asset classes.

## Outcome

For three years I helped run software where the stakes were measured in billions, moving
institutional capital across global markets in milliseconds. The work taught me a kind of
architecture — composable, model-driven, built for speed and precision — and a kind of
discipline: staying technically rigorous and always accountable while being fully present
for a young family at home. When the arrangement wound down in 2003, the industry was
already shifting to C# and .NET, and I moved on to learn it.
