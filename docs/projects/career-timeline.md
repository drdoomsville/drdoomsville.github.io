# Career Timeline

---

**Microsoft** *(1st stint)*
Started at the top — supporting MS-DOS, Word, Windows NT, and shipping automated UI tests for Visual Basic 4.0 at the company that was literally defining what personal computing looked like.

---

**Transamerica**
Built TARSUS, a custom VB reinsurance application that turned mind-bending insurance risk calculations into something a computer could actually manage — first real taste of enterprise software's dirty secret: the business logic is always more complicated than anyone admits.

---

**InfoSystem** *(North Carolina)*
Won Microsoft Partner of the Year building one of the most advanced retail POS systems of 1995 — then won Rookie of the Year personally — then left, because winning awards at a company that treats its people poorly is its own kind of education.

---

**SMCI / Duke Energy**
First contracting engagement: e-learning systems and PeopleSoft migrations for a multi-state regulated utility, where the real skill wasn't technical — it was learning how to move a mammoth-sized organization one careful step at a time.

---

**Rabar Market Research**
A hedge fund run by one of Richard Dennis's original Turtles shipped a $20,000 server and a satellite dish to the front door — and Sonny spent three years building a composable C++ algorithmic trading system watching nearly a billion dollars move across fifty global markets, from a home office, with a young family in the next room.

---

**SDS Labs**
A focused, precise engagement building a blood-test results ingestion and transformation utility — the kind of quiet, unglamorous work where getting it exactly right matters more than anyone will ever notice.

---

**Microsoft** *(2nd stint)*
Returned to Microsoft as a developer support escalation specialist for C++ and early .NET — the job was walking into a stranger's broken code and understanding it faster than they expected, which turned out to be one of the most transferable skills in the career.

---

**First Union**
Unified a call center system built from decades of mismatched layers — console apps, Smalltalk, COBOL — into a single coherent Windows interface, where the hardest part wasn't the code, it was convincing people to trust the new thing over the old chaos they'd memorized.

---

**BCF**
Built a real-time insurance quote aggregation platform anchored to ACORD XML before Progressive made the idea famous — the product was acquired, the founders cashed out, and the ten developers who built it were handed a pay cut.

---

**Bank of America** *(trading floor)*
Paired with a former NASA rocket engineer from Skunk Works to automate defeasance — a recursive optimization problem that ran three hundred levels deep to perfectly balance T-bill portfolios against $236 billion in commercial loans, with a team of exactly two people.

---

**Wachovia**
Built the .NET routing and status layer on top of LoanIQ after the First Union merger created one of the era's largest banking institutions — unglamorous infrastructure work that had to be exactly right, every single day.

---

**Roundpoint Mortgage**
Ran the full gamut simultaneously: capital markets file parsing, nightly batch processes, Fidelity green-screen automations, accounting tools, a public website with an India contractor team, and software for three internal divisions — the engagement where context-switching became a core competency.

---

**Pegasystems** *(consulting — multiple clients)*
Certified as a Pega Systems Architect and deployed across six cities and six industries over several years — the highlights being a hundred-consultant CIBC onboarding overhaul, T-Mobile's IMEI-level phone repair vendor system, and the US Census Bureau's 2020 census platform that cut the national canvassing workforce by more than fifty percent.

---

**Ally**
Compliance and fraud-detection RPA with Automation Anywhere, plus C#/.NET utilities for an adjacent team — ended at the flashpoint of a seventy-thousand-record migration that escalated beyond its scope and made the exit decision easy.

---

**Wells Fargo**
Stepped into the solution architect seat — going business-facing to discover, map, and document processes clearly enough to hand to engineering teams — a role that only becomes credible after decades of being the one building the thing.

---

**AvidXchange**
Senior manager over the team running approximately $50 billion per year in nightly transaction processing across 1.5 million vendors — where the measure of success was invisible when everything worked, and immediately, painfully obvious when it didn't.

---

**Merck** *(pending)*
The next chapter is still being written.
