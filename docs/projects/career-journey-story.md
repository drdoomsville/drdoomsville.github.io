# The Long Game
### A Career in Software — Executive Summary

---

Thirty-plus years. Seventeen companies. Fifty billion dollars in nightly transactions. Half a million census contractors reduced to half that. A NASA rocket scientist's algorithm. A trading system watching a billion dollars in global futures. A POS platform that won Microsoft Partner of the Year before most developers had heard of 32-bit Windows.

No single industry. No single stack. One constant: walk into the hard room, figure it out fast, and build something that lasts.

---

## Microsoft — Where It Started

The career opened at the top. Supporting MS-DOS 5.0, Word, Windows NT, and Microsoft Scenes gave an early education in software at scale — what it meant to build products used by millions, and what broke when they did. The role that left the deepest mark was SDET on Visual Basic 4.0: designing automated UI test frameworks for classroom teacher-student environments. An early lesson that quality isn't a phase at the end — it's an architecture decision from the start.

---

## Transamerica — Enterprise, Up Close

At Transamerica, the work was TARSUS: a custom Visual Basic reinsurance application built from scratch to manage the financial mechanics of distributing insurance risk across carriers. The first real encounter with enterprise software's defining truth — the business logic is always more complicated than the spec, and the data is always messier than anyone admits up front. Getting it right required learning the domain as thoroughly as the code.

---

## InfoSystem — The Year of 70-Hour Weeks

1995. North Carolina. A POS system for Petco — their first ever — plus Eagles and Giant Eagle, eventually running across twenty-plus stores in five retail chains. The technology was genuinely ahead of its time: 32-bit Visual Basic, MS SQL, OLE/COM object architecture. The team grew from eight developers to fifteen. The project won **Microsoft Partner of the Year for Retail**.

The cost: seventy hours a week, most of the year. Sonny won Rookie of the Year. Then he left — not because of the workload, but because of what he watched. How the company treated the people who built the award-winning product. How it treated its customers. The most valuable lessons from that year weren't on the trophy — they were in understanding exactly how *not* to lead.

He started contracting. The right call.

---

## SMCI / Duke Energy — First Contracts, Enterprise Scale

The contracting years opened at Duke Energy via SMCI — a regulated utility operating at a scale where every project required threading an organizational needle. Online course development, computer-based training delivery, PeopleSoft migrations, and a set of database and VB tools that kept the infrastructure running. An education in how large enterprises actually move: deliberately, carefully, and with stakeholder management as a core technical skill.

---

## Rabar Market Research — A Billion Dollars and a Satellite Dish

UPS delivered a $20,000 workstation server and a financial satellite dish to the front door. That's how Rabar Market Research equipped its remote team, because the work demanded nothing less.

Paul Rabar was one of Richard Dennis's original Turtles — the 1980s trading experiment that proved discipline could be taught. He built a quantitative hedge fund around that conviction, eventually managing nearly a billion dollars across fifty-plus global futures and currency markets at nearly 10x leverage.

The software Sonny built was the engine: a C++ composable trading system where models could be swapped in and out like plugins, multiple running in parallel, each calibrated to different market conditions. Every night, the system identified the three hundred most liquid assets. The highest-velocity ones became the next day's targets. Human analysts could override any trade — but the system was the primary actor.

The personal challenge matched the technical one. Sonny ran all of this from home, with a young family, in an era before remote work had a playbook. The discipline that required — performing at institutional level while staying present as a husband and father — was its own kind of achievement. In 2003, a recession gave Rabar reason to end remote arrangements. A natural exit. The next move: learn C# and .NET.

---

## SDS Labs — Precision in a Small Package

A focused engagement, not a headline one. SDS Labs needed a blood-test results utility: ingest, transform, output. Healthcare data at its most precise — formatted correctly, validated thoroughly, delivered reliably. The kind of project that doesn't make news and doesn't need to. It works, or it doesn't.

It worked.

## Microsoft — The Second Time

The second Microsoft stint was diagnostic, not generative. Developer support escalations across C++ and the emerging .NET platform — when developers had problems that couldn't be resolved through standard channels, they came here. Reading unfamiliar code under pressure, understanding intent, identifying failure, explaining the fix. It sharpened a particular skill that would prove invaluable for the rest of the career: entering any codebase cold and getting oriented fast.

---

## First Union — Taming the Patchwork

First Union's call center was decades of technology layered on top of itself: console applications, Smalltalk components, COBOL backends. Each layer rational when it was added; collectively, a maze that agents navigated through institutional memory. The mission was a unified Windows interface that brought it all together without forcing a full replacement of any piece. The technical problem was tractable. The organizational one — earning trust from people who'd worked around the old system for years — was the actual job.

---

## BCF — Before Progressive

BCF was thirty-five people who spotted a gap before the insurance industry did. When a broker needed quotes from multiple carriers, they re-entered customer information from scratch, every time, for every carrier. BCF's platform took it once and distributed it in real time — anchored to the ACORD XML standard for the clean integrations, and screen scraping for the carriers whose web technology hadn't caught up yet.

A few years later, Progressive made this exact idea famous. BCF was already building it.

The acquisition came — believed to be Vertafore. The three founders cashed out well. The ten developers who built it were offered a pay cut to stay. Most left. The startup energy had been real — fast, close, purposeful. The exit made clear that energy and equity are different things.

---

## Bank of America — The Rocket Scientist's Problem

A former NASA rocket engineer named George Russell had spent the 1970s at Lockheed's Skunk Works working on control systems and flight trajectories. Then he noticed what banks paid. He switched industries, brought his optimization instincts with him, and needed one thing: someone who could code his idea.

The idea was defeasance — automating the process of replacing commercial loan collateral with a precisely engineered portfolio of T-bills, T-notes, and T-bonds whose scheduled payments cover monthly loan obligations within a narrow threshold. Not too little (penalties). Not too much (tax implications). Exactly right — for the entire life of the loan, across a dynamically changing principal balance.

The codebase was lean. The algorithm was a recursive optimizer running two to three hundred levels deep. The loan portfolio it ran against: $236 billion. Team size: two.

A rocket scientist with the right idea and a programmer who could execute it can move things that look immovable.

---

## Wachovia — Infrastructure After the Merger

First Union became Wachovia through one of the era's largest banking mergers. On the other side of it: a .NET routing and status layer built on top of LoanIQ, the commercial lending platform used to manage large corporate loan portfolios. The proposed architecture included a message-driven state machine and a reflection-and-generics abstraction layer designed to keep the system flexible as requirements evolved. Banking infrastructure at this scale is invisible when done correctly. That was the goal.

---

## Roundpoint Mortgage — The Full Portfolio

Roundpoint wasn't one project — it was everything at once. Capital markets utilities for parsing and transforming mortgage tranche files. Nightly finance batch processes. Fidelity green-screen automations bridging legacy to modern. Accounting automations. Network integrations to outside companies. A public-facing website managed with a contractor team in India. And software serving three distinct internal divisions: servicing, originations, and lead generation — each with its own stakeholders and its own definition of done.

The engagement required someone who could hold multiple workstreams simultaneously, operate independently, and be trusted to make the right call without supervision. That's exactly what Roundpoint got.

## Pegasystems — The Consulting Years

Pega was a Boston-based enterprise software firm built around a powerful platform: low-code, highly configurable workflow automation, the "situational layered cake." Getting certified — weeks of training, formal examination — meant becoming a Pega Systems Architect and getting placed on a client. From there, the role was never just one thing. Developer, requirements analyst, change request manager, support desk, and client-facing voice — often in the same day.

**CIBC (Toronto):** Over a hundred consultants rebuilding customer onboarding across web, branch, and phone channels for one of Canada's largest banks — with real-time AI decisioning for retention, campaigns, and personalized offers. One year. One flight per week.

**T-Mobile (Seattle):** Twenty vendors. Hundreds of locations. Millions of phones tracked by IMEI through triage, disassembly, refurbishment, and redistribution. Previously managed by Excel spreadsheets. Not anymore.

**Sprint (Kansas):** Campaign management infrastructure for promotional phone offers — the workflow engine behind how Sprint structured and delivered new customer incentives at scale.

**US Census Bureau (Washington, DC):** The flagship engagement. Starting in 2016, the team built the technology stack that powered the 2020 census — Google Maps property-change detection, multi-channel survey delivery (online, mail, IVR), and a manager-and-canvasser mobile platform with GPS tracking, address mapping, and multilingual survey delivery. The result: the contractor workforce required to canvass the country dropped from five to six hundred thousand people to under three hundred thousand. More than a fifty percent reduction — built into software extensible enough to carry into future censuses without starting over.

**The RPA chapter:** Pega acquired a .NET-based robotic process automation product and used it as a foot-in-the-door sales strategy — sell RPA, then upsell the expensive PRPC platform once the client was committed. RPA itself was genuinely useful for bounded problems where no API existed. The misuse was in pushing it as a developer replacement. Corporations handed automations to people without engineering fundamentals and got brittle, unlogged failures dressed up as automation. Additional engagements included Bank of America (project management), BDP International (real-time shipping container tracking), and Blue Cross Blue Shield, where a brilliant but overreaching attempt to build an Excel-to-RPA workflow translator took over a year and never fully shipped. Some ideas are right in principle and impossible at scale. This was one of them.

## Ally — Automation, Fraud, and a Clean Exit

At Ally, the work split across compliance RPA and fraud-detection automation built with Automation Anywhere, plus C#/.NET utilities for an adjacent team. The engagement ended at a flashpoint: a seventy-thousand-record one-off data migration that escalated beyond its original scope. Some exits are clean. Others teach you something about where the lines are. This one was the latter.

---

## Wells Fargo — The Architect's Seat

Wells Fargo brought a shift in posture: solution architecture in a business-facing role. The work was process discovery, mapping, and documentation — going into business units, understanding what existed and what needed to exist, and producing specifications clear enough to hand to engineering teams. A role that only becomes possible after decades of being the one holding the wrench. You have to have built the thing before you can credibly tell others where to aim.

---

## AvidXchange — Leading at Scale

The stakes got concrete at AvidXchange: approximately **$50 billion per year** in nightly transaction processing, flowing through automated, semi-automated, and check-based payment paths to roughly **1.5 million vendors**. When it ran correctly, it was invisible. When it didn't, the consequences were immediate.

Sonny's role was senior management over the team keeping it running — building a team capable of operating reliably under pressure, diagnosing failures fast, and understanding the weight of what they were responsible for. Not just technical leadership. Organizational leadership. The kind that only works when the people in the room trust that you've seen what they're seeing, and built your way through it.

## The Through-Line

Retail floors and trading desks. A satellite dish and a recursive bond optimizer. A census that counted America. A mortgage servicer and a claims automation that never shipped. Fifty billion dollars a night.

The career doesn't fit a box — and that's the point.

What it adds up to is rare: someone who has operated at depth across finance, government, healthcare, retail, telecom, and technology; who has led teams and written code; who has been the architect and the developer and the consultant and the manager; who has seen technology misused often enough to know what right actually looks like.

The lessons that stick aren't technical. They're about people. How the best teams operate. What leadership actually costs. Why the difference between a company that wins *with* its people and one that wins *despite* them is the most important variable in any project.

Thirty-plus years. Still building. Still learning. Still walking into the hard room.
