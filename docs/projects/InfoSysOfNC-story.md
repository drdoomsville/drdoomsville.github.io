# The Year of 70-Hour Weeks

In 1995, retail technology was still figuring itself out. Most stores ran on clunky, proprietary systems that barely talked to each other. Into that gap stepped a small company called Infosystem of NC, with a bold bet: build a modern, multi-chain point-of-sale system on cutting-edge 32-bit Visual Basic, a Microsoft SQL backend, and OLE/COM object architecture — technology so advanced for its time that most developers hadn't touched it yet.

I was brought onto the project mid-stream, initially just to lend a hand. But within a day, it was clear this wasn't a short-term favor — the project was bigger, more complex, and more urgent than anyone had let on. The team needed someone who could operate at the system's level. Thus I was tapped to stay.

---

## Building Something special, one was the team and other was the product

The codebase they were building wasn't just a cash register app. It was a sophisticated n-tier application — close to 5,000 lines — designed to run across multiple retail chains simultaneously. Petco was deploying it as their **first-ever POS system**. Eagles and Giant Eagle grocery stores were on the rollout list too. By the time the year was out, the system would be live in over 20 stores across more than five separate chains.

For 1995, this was remarkable. Multi-tenant retail software, running on a unified architecture, deployed at that scale? The industry hadn't seen much like it.

The team reflected the ambition. When Sonny joined, there were 8 developers. By year's end, they'd nearly doubled to 15. And somewhere in that growth, I was promoted to Sr Developer which translated to the **#2 developer on the project** — the technical second-in-command of one of the most exciting retail software efforts at the time.

---

## The Cost 

In 1995, Infosystem's POS solution won the **Microsoft Partner of the Year Award for the Retail Industry** — a recognition greatly appreciated by the whole team; but the award did not capture what it actually cost to get there.

The developers on team averaged very close **70 hours a week** for most eveyr week of that year. Not occasionally. Consistently. Week after week, the work expanded to fill every available hour — and then some. The technical achievement was real, but it was being built on the backs of people who were quietly burning out. Most of us hit 40 hours Monday and miday on Wednesday, week in & week out. 

I was recognized for my contributions. I won **Rookie of the Year** on the technical side of the business — an acknowledgment that what he brought to the team mattered, and that his growth over that year.

---

## What the Award Didn't Say

But there's a difference between a company that succeeds *with* its people and one that succeeds *despite* them.

I watched how this small corporation operated — how it treated the employees grinding out those 70-hour weeks, how it treated customers. The ethical practices I observed didn't match my personal principles. A company can win and still get the fundamentals wrong.

That realization — hard-earned, firsthand — became one of the most important lessons of his career:

> *How not to treat employees.*

Success metrics are easy to chase. Culture is harder. And when culture is broken, no award changes what it feels like to show up every day.

---

## A New Chapter

So I chose to leave and begin the next chapter of my professional life.

He started contracting.

It was the first time. And it wouldn't be the last.
